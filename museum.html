The Museum

A curated journey through Lebanon’s monetary heritage — from Phoenician silver to the digital future.
A story told in coins, in memories, and now… in FRANGAIN™.

Room I — The Phoenician Room

Long before Lebanon had a name, its shores minted stories in silver. Phoenician cities like Tyre, Sidon, and Byblos produced coins portraying ships, kings, and gods. These early coins were more than currency — they were cultural signatures, symbols of maritime power, and the earliest pulse of a people destined to trade with the world.

Room II — The Roman Room

Under Roman rule, Lebanon’s economy was woven into the empire’s golden machine. Imperial denarii and aurei flowed through Baalbek and Beirut. Local mints produced coins featuring emperors, eagles, temples — reminders that the region was a vital link in Rome’s eastern provinces. Money was propaganda, power, and prestige.

Room III — The Arab-Islamic Room

The Arab-Islamic period introduced dinars and dirhams, marked by calligraphy instead of images. For the first time, currency became a visual language of faith and unity. These coins traveled across continents — proof that Lebanon’s ports remained open doors to economic life and cultural exchange.

Room IV — The Ottoman Room

Under the Ottomans, the Majidi (medjidiye) became a household coin across the Levant. Lebanese families saved them, traded them, gifted them during weddings — a tradition that still echoes in stories today. The economy was fragile, but the coin was familiar, trusted, part of memory.

Room V — The Lebanese Lira Room (1930s–1980s)

The birth of the modern Lebanese Lira brought stability and pride. Coins and banknotes included:

• **خمسة قروش** (5 Piastres) • **عشرة قروش** (10 Piastres) • 25 Piastres — Quarter of a Lira • 50 Piastres — Half a Lira • 1 Lira coin • 5 Liras — banknote • 10 Liras — banknote • 50 Liras — banknote • 100 Liras — later also a coin • 250 Liras — banknote then coin • 500 Liras — banknote then coin

This was the golden era of Lebanese confidence — when the Lira was strong, respected, and part of daily life.

Room VI — The Folklore Room

Not all currency exists as metal. Some exist as memory. The “Frang” lived not as a specific coin, but as a cultural expression — a slang carried by families, merchants, and storytellers. It became folklore: a symbol of modest transactions, childhood memories, and a Lebanon that felt simpler.

Room VII — The Collapse Room (1980s & 2019)

Lebanon experienced two major monetary collapses:

**• The first:** Early 1980s — when the Lira lost its anchor during conflict and inflation. **• The second:** 2019–today — the unraveling of a century-old financial system, the disappearance of coins and small notes, and the rise of chaos in daily purchasing power.

Currency became a victim of history.

Room VIII — The Future Room: FRANGAIN™

And now begins another chapter. FRANGAIN™ is not a replacement — it is a remembrance. A digital echo of the “Frang” that passed through generations. Not built to speculate, but to preserve. Not created for hype, but for history.

The coin remembers. And through FRANGAIN™, the memory rises again.



A token of memory, not speculation.
